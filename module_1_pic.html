💡 Why this design?

A flat checklist would have implied competencies are binary — "you have it or you don't." The 4×3×2 structure recognises three things educators actually experience: different domains require different skills, growth happens progressively, and your own use ≠ teaching others to use.

1.2.2 · Tips for Contextualizing the Questions

The Same Question Looks Different in Different Settings

A competency statement like "guide students to critically engage with GenAI" plays out very differently in a 12-person clinical seminar versus a 300-person engineering lecture, and differently again across cultures. Here are three context layers to keep in mind.

Three layers shape how the same competency translates into your specific classroom: your discipline's epistemology, your learners' profile, and the cultural conventions of your institution and region. None of these is a "barrier" — each is a translation key.

Disciplines have different epistemologies, different relationships with authoritative texts, and very different conventions for "what counts as student work." A philosophy essay and an engineering lab report face GenAI in fundamentally different ways.

📌 Same question, different disciplines — "How should students cite GenAI use in their final project?"
HistoryDetailed transparency about prompts & outputs; emphasis on source verification.
EngineeringCode-comment-style attribution; reproducibility takes precedence over narrative.
Creative WritingDistinction between AI-generated drafts vs. human-revised text matters most.
Clinical HealthPatient-safety context shapes everything; some uses may be inappropriate at any level.

Class size, year level, prior preparation, and language background all reshape what's pedagogically possible. A Year 1 cohort of 250 needs different scaffolds than a Master's seminar of 8.

📌 Same goal, different learners — "Help students critically evaluate GenAI outputs."
Year 1, n=250Worked-example videos + low-stakes weekly quizzes; structured handouts.
Year 3, n=80Peer "fact-check the chatbot" tasks; co-designed evaluation rubrics.
Master's, n=12Open seminar critique; students design their own GenAI workflows.
EAL-rich cohortExplicit modelling of evaluative language; bilingual prompt examples.

Norms around authority, originality, citation, and what counts as "knowledge work" differ across institutional and cultural contexts. The framework's competency statements need translation, not just transfer.

📌 One competency, different cultural readings — "Encourage students to question the reliability of outputs."
Context AMay map naturally to existing classroom norms of open critique.
Context BMay require explicit framing to legitimise critique of authoritative-sounding text.
Multilingual settingOutput quality & bias differ across languages — requires localised examples.
Mixed cohortMake assumptions visible; invite students to surface their own conventions.

📎 Second tip — learn with specific evidence

Don't generalise. Anchor every reflection in concrete evidence:

  • A specific lesson plan, syllabus, or rubric you've used.
  • A concrete student artefact (with appropriate ethics & privacy).
  • A real prompt & output exchange — not a hypothetical.
  • A specific incident: a question a student asked, a moment that surprised you.
Why this matters: When you bring real evidence into the modules ahead, your AI companion can give you grounded feedback rather than generic advice — and you'll leave with insights that fit your classroom, not someone else's.

1.3.1 · Workshop Mechanics at a Glance

Six Things to Remember

📚
Sequential modules, free pacing

Modules 1–6 are designed to flow in order, but you choose when to engage and how long to spend on each.

✓
Mark sections complete

Use the "Mark Complete" button at the end of each section to track your progress in the navigation bar.

💬
AI companion is a thinking partner

The chatbot won't grade you or give "the right answer" — it asks questions to help you reflect more clearly.

🎯
Pop quizzes & recalls — not graded

Interactive checks help you self-assess. Change your answer anytime; nothing is recorded as a "score".

📝
Reflections & practice tasks

Open-ended prompts where you describe your own context. Honest answers produce better AI feedback.

🌏
Different educators in each module

Modules 2–5 introduce educators from varied disciplines so you see the framework across contexts.
