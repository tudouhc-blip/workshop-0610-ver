2.1.1 · The Practical Challenge

Why GenAI Literacy Has Become Urgent

Traditional digital literacy prepared us to evaluate a webpage or judge a source. GenAI introduces a different challenge: outputs look fluent and authoritative — even when they're wrong. Teachers face this twice over: in their own work, and in guiding students who are already using these tools.

⚠️ DR. CHEN'S MOMENT

"Last semester, two of my students submitted essays with citations to studies that don't exist. I didn't catch it — a TA did. Honestly, I'm not sure I could teach my students to spot AI errors when I'm not always confident I could spot them myself."

— Dr. Maya Chen, before starting this workshop

2.1.2 · Definitions

What This Framework Means by "GenAI Literacy"

You've probably encountered "AI literacy" before — usually framed in technical terms. In this framework, we mean something broader and more pedagogically grounded.

CONVENTIONAL AI LITERACY
  • Technical know-how
  • Tool operation
  • Individual skill
  • An end in itself
GENAI LITERACY (THIS FRAMEWORK)
  • Critical, ethical, applied use
  • Pedagogical judgement
  • Foundation for all dimensions
  • A starting point, not a destination
Two perspectives are always at play: your own literacy as a teacher, and your capacity to empower students' literacy. These are related but distinct — strength in one does not guarantee strength in the other.

2.4.1 · Key Takeaways, Best Practices & Strategies

Six Insights Specific to GenAI Literacy

These takeaways aren't general study tips — they're the core ideas specific to GenAI Literacy as a dimension. Each is anchored to where it appeared in the module, so you can return to the source if you want to go deeper.

FRAMEWORK CORE
GenAI Literacy is two perspectives, not one

Your own literacy and your capacity to empower students' literacy are related but distinct. Strength in one does not guarantee strength in the other.

From §2.1.2: "Two perspectives are always at play."
OWN LITERACY
Critical engagement is what separates Basic from Intermediate

The shift up isn't about using more tools — it's about iteratively refining prompts AND verifying outputs against trusted sources, while watching for bias and framing issues.

From §2.2.1: Maya's Intermediate scenario.
OWN LITERACY
Advanced means GenAI as cognitive partner — and sharing it

At Advanced, you're not just using GenAI well; you're designing novel workflows (e.g., "devil's advocate" co-thinking) and contributing those insights to a professional learning community.

From §2.2.1: Maya's Advanced scenario.
EMPOWERING STUDENTS
Basic-level student work is about safety scaffolds, not restrictions

"Outputs are drafts, not facts." A Basic-level student briefing introduces GenAI through clear prompts, privacy guidelines, and the habit of questioning reliability — not through prohibitions.

From §2.3.1: Maya's Weeks 1–4 with students.
EMPOWERING STUDENTS
Co-designing rubrics is the Intermediate hinge

When students help build the criteria for evaluating AI outputs, they move from passive consumers to critical interlocutors. This single move signals the Intermediate shift in the empowerment track.

From §2.3.1: Maya's Weeks 5–9 with students.
FOUNDATION
This dimension underpins the next three

Without GenAI Literacy, you cannot make informed choices in Curriculum & Learning Design, Teaching & Learning, or Assessment. It is the precondition — not just the first step.

From §2.1.3: The dimension diagram.
🛠 BEST PRACTICES & STRATEGIES YOU CAN APPLY TOMORROW

Five Concrete Moves Drawn From This Module

  1. Verify before you use: Run every GenAI-generated citation through your library database before it touches a slide or a syllabus.
  2. Iterate, don't accept: Treat the first output as a draft. Refine your prompt at least once — explicitly asking for diverse regions, contested data, or alternative framings.
  3. Co-design rubrics with students: Spend one class building the evaluation criteria together. This single move activates critical engagement across the rest of the semester.
  4. Adopt a class mantra: "Outputs are drafts, not facts." Repeat it until students self-monitor without prompting.
  5. Build a co-thinking workflow: Use GenAI to generate counterarguments to your own framing — then bring those into class as a "devil's advocate" segment.
